19:32 - 20:09

While Americans of all races struggled with unemployment in the early 1980s, the problem was particularly stark for Black Americans. According to Federal Reserve data, white unemployment was 9.1% in January 1983 (the year of this telecast), while Black unemployment rose to 21.2%.

Historical Background

20:55 - 21:12

 In 1965, after the Voting Rights Act returned the franchise to African Americans, Julian Bond was elected to the US House of Representatives—the first Black congressmen from Georgia since Reconstruction. He continued to serve in the House for six terms, and in the 1990s, became the Chair of the NAACP.

Who is talking?

21:29 - 21:45

Here, economist Alan Reynolds, who became a fellow at the libertarian Cato Institute, presents a conservative argument about urban unemployment in this era. According to this interpretation, Black city residents were not unable to find jobs, but made a “voluntary lifestyle” choice not to pursue employment. To the extent structural forces were involved, conservative sociologists like Charles Murray argued that overly generous and poorly designed welfare programs provided a disincentive to work.
