The Middle Class Exodus

Historical Context

09:53

As this section implies, the postwar period saw a boom in automobile ownership. By 1930, over 40% of American households owned a car, often purchased from Henry Ford or other American car companies. Car ownership stagnated during the Great Depression and World War II, before growing dramatically in the postwar period. By 1960, more than three in four households owned a car; over 20% owned more than one.

10:20

This portion of the clip illustrates what became a regular part of millions of American lives in the postwar period: the daily automobile commute to work. Government policy was central in creating this reality. Across the country--and perhaps most famously in New York City under legendary urban planner Robert Moses--municipal governments tore down sections of existing cities to make room for highways connecting suburbs to urban cores. In Milwaukee, where this clip is set, 7,500 housing units were torn down in this era as part of various "urban renewal" projects.

10:48

Indeed, the rise of the automobile (and accompanying automobile infrastructure) was a major force facilitating suburbanization. So too was federal housing policy. The GI Bill made it far easier for World War II veterans (a major portion of the population) to secure a mortgage on a new home. And New Deal-era Federal Housing Authority also incentivized new home construction outside the urban core. The FHA insured home mortgages, but not in certain neighborhoods that were deemed "high-risk"--because of the color of these neighborhoods on FHA maps, this process became known as "redlining." As a result of this policy, it was difficult to impossible to secure mortgages in certain urban neighborhoods, but easier to get insured loans in the suburbs. Thus, federal housing policy help attract the growing middle-class out of the city.
