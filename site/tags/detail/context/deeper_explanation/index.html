1. The Middle Class Exodus

Context
Deeper Explanation

10:20

This portion of the clip illustrates what became a regular part of millions of American lives in the postwar period: the daily automobile commute to work. Government policy was central in creating this reality. Across the country--and perhaps most famously in New York City under legendary urban planner Robert Moses--municipal governments tore down sections of existing cities to make room for highways connecting suburbs to urban cores. In Milwaukee, where this clip is set, 7,500 housing units were torn down in this era as part of various "urban renewal" projects.

10. The “Local Control” Argument Against Aid to Cities (1977)

Context
Deeper Explanation

16:20

Host Jim Lehrer provides one example of why regional revenue sharing might lead to “perverse incentives”: a bloated, inefficient, city government might become dependent on outside financial aid rather than make the kind of fiscal reforms needed to become self-sufficient. Another “perverse incentive” that someone like Hawkins might identify is that cities would not take the necessary steps to promote new economic development in the central city. If a major city would receive some tax revenue even if a new business was established in the suburbs, there might be less incentive for cities to take the steps to attract new businesses back into the city. In the long run, this could contribute to continued economic stagnation.

Context
Deeper Explanation

18:57

Here, Hawkins explains another key argument used by opponents of regional revenue sharing programs: the principle of “local control.” The comparison to France is instructive. Unlike many European nation-states, in which national governments hold most of the decision-making power (and local governments largely exist to carry out national government mandates) more power is “devolved” to the state and local levels in the USA. States, counties, and cities exercise distinct taxing and lawmaking powers. The principle that “the government closest to the people serves the people best” (often attributed to Thomas Jefferson) aligned well with the views of suburban residents who thought it unfair and unwise to cede tax revenue to nearby cities.

11. Debating Aid to Struggling Cities (1978)

Context
Deeper Explanation

21:55

“CETA” refers to the Comprehensive Employment and Training Act, a law passed during the Nixon Administration that facilitated job training and public sector jobs for people in need of work, such as low-income high school students or the long-term unemployed. While the program wasn’t specifically an urban program, many of the jobs did go to impoverished residents of struggling cities in this era. Here, “counter-cyclical aid” refers to federal government aid given to cities during economic downturns, like what the US experienced in the mid-1970s. The logic was that local governments lost revenue during times of economic hardship (e.g., declining consumer spending led to lower sales tax revenue) but still needed to provide services to residents. Federal counter-cyclical aid could fill the fiscal gaps that might emerge as the result of a recession.

Context
Deeper Explanation

22:05

Host Robert MacNeil’s reference to urban governments as “junkies” who suffered from “dependency” on outside aid channels a perspective held by Americans who were critical of “bailouts” to struggling cities. According to this point of view, to the extent certain cities were in economic crisis, they only had themselves to blame: municipal government spending was out of control; party machines controlled local governments and were corruption was rampant; public sector unions held politicians hostage and made it politically difficult to make necessary cuts to government workers. Thus, federal aid was futile or even counterproductive, since it enabled city governments to continue their bad habits rather than making necessary reforms.

Context
Deeper Explanation

22:24

Most cities collect revenue through sales taxes (collecting a percentage of the value of purchases made at local stores) and property taxes (collecting a tax proportionate to the assessed value of a home or other piece of property). During the urban crisis of the 1970s, these streams proved insufficient. Sales tax revenue was declining as residents had less money to spend, and property tax revenue plummeted as more affluent property owners moved to the suburbs. Additionally, as Mayor Gibson notes, a particularly high proportion of Newark property was untaxable because it was owned by federal or state government (e.g. the port authority). Most states in the US, including New Jersey at this time, do not allow local governments to collect income tax, shutting off another potential revenue stream. Eventually, Newark was allowed to start collecting a 1% income tax from local employers.

12. The Political Challenge of Reviving New Jersey’s Cities (1982)

Context
Deeper Explanation

19:56

The city of Camden joined Newark as one of the prime examples of urban blight in New Jersey. In the early 20th century, Camden was a major industrial center with a major shipbuilding industry and thousands of manufacturing jobs at firms like the Campbell’s Soup Company and the electronics company, RCA. Like so many Northeastern cities, deindustrialization and the flight of white middle-class residents hit Camden hard in the 1960s and 1970s. Between 1950 and 1980, the city’s population fell by more than 30% from around 125,000 to 85,000, and the number of manufacturing faced a similarly steep downward trajectory. The construction of a major prison in Camden provides a sad irony; desperate for money, Camden took on a state project that was arguably made necessary by the rise in crime in decaying cities like Camden.

Context
Deeper Explanation

21:04

Across the United States during a time of growing inflation, there was mounting frustration with taxation. The passage of the famous “Prop 13” referendum in California capped property tax rates in that state and placed the steep requirement that any tax increase required a two-thirds majority in both houses of the state legislature. Rather than directly limit property tax levels, the New Jersey legislature passed a law intended to stifle public spending: county, school, and municipal governments were not allowed to increase their budgets by more than 5% in any given year.

Context
Deeper Explanation

21:55

In the 1970s, the Federal government provided significant funding to municipal governments to help them deal with the fiscal challenges of the economic stagnation that had hit cities particularly hard. After Ronald Reagan’s victory in the 1980 election, federal policy moved in a different direction. Under what became known as “New Federalism,” Reagan argued that more government programs should be funded and operated at the local and state level, with the federal government playing a smaller role in funding these efforts. The resulting changes in policy led to declines in federal “block grant” programs, in which local governments and organizations used federal money to fund community development and services programs. Critics argued that this change denied necessary federal aid to cities at the time they needed it most; deindustrialization and suburbanization undermined the budgets of urban centers at the same time as struggling urban residents were most in need of government-funded social services.

2. The Struggles of the Auto Industry

Context
Deeper Explanation

15:31

As the interviewee indicates, longer-tenured workers often kept their jobs during layoffs because union contracts often protected laborers based on seniority with “last hired first fired" provisions. However, this meant a generation of younger workers lost their auto jobs early in their career; and furthermore, a generation of young men who would have gotten steady union jobs had they came of age in the 1950s were instead left struggling to find unemployment because they entered in the workforce in the “hard times” of the late 1970s

Context
Deeper Explanation

15:54

This section shows why even the “stabilized” US auto industry of the 1980s would never employ as many workers as it had during its mid-century height. While the cars produced by GM, Chrysler and Ford were technically “American-made,” many of the “pieces” of a car were actually produced in other countries. Between this globalization of the car supply chain and the automation discussed in the clip, far fewer American workers were needed to produce the same number of cars as would have been needed a generation before. This had devastating impacts on the economies of Detroit and other cities which relied on the tax revenue and consumer spending that came from employment in the auto industry.

Context
Deeper Explanation

17:29

This is a reference to the federal government bailout of Chrysler in 1979. The company was facing bankruptcy, and executives argued that the country had an interest in saving the jobs of Chrysler employees and protecting an American company from losing out to foreign competition. Ultimately, the federal government gave $1.5 billion in loans to Chrysler (which was ultimately paid back with interest).

Context
Deeper Explanation

18:05

The discussion within this section illuminates the debates happening within unions in the late 1970s and early 1980s. Many workers felt that they were in a “catch-22” situation: if they fought company efforts to pursue globalization of the supply chain and automation, jobs might be saved, but the company might also go out of busines. If workers accepted these reforms, some jobs would be lost, but hopefully the company would stay in business and some jobs would be saved. In some cases, union negotiators made concessions to management that led to layoffs in order to maintain industry competitiveness, which led to some “rank and file” resentment of leadership. In the view of these critics, the union should be fighting harder rather than accepting the “management line” about the need to outsource certain parts of the production process.

5. Urban Decay in the South Bronx

Context
Deeper Explanation

21:54

The interviewee is a “squatter” who stayed in an empty apartment without paying rent. “Squatting” was common in the South Bronx in the late 1970s. With so many abandoned, burned-out apartments, there were many places for poor people to live if they were willing to live without heat, water, and electricity. According to a 2015 Time Magazine article, the police were far more concerned with more serious crime, and generally made little effort to evict people from abandoned buildings.

Context
Deeper Explanation

22:52

Sometimes landlords were the culprits in the rash of arsons in the South Bronx in the late 1970s because an insurance settlement paid better than collecting rent. According to Moyers’ narration in a different section, “You can buy a large occupied apartment house in the Bronx for less than a thousand dollars. By taking advantage of the city's three-year tax moratorium, you can collect several thousand a month while paying no taxes; provide heat and services infrequently and only under duress; no maintenance. A few promises will keep the rents coming in until the tenants give up in disgust. Then $200 will buy you a first-class arson job. Federally- subsidized fire insurance is required by law. A quick settlement, with few questions, puts you ahead by $70- or $80,000.”

Context
Deeper Explanation

24:01

Sometimes residents set these fires. Naturally, some fires were accidental, but others were deliberate. Earlier in the documentary, Moyers explains that there were long waiting lists for public housing but people whose apartments were burned down received higher priority—thus creating an incentive to burn your own residence if you were already living in poor conditions. Additionally, Moyers’ reporting describes a family who “received a little over $2,000 for new clothing and furniture and were moved to a new apartment at public expense.” According to a police detective he interviewed, this policy encouraged families to burn their own homes to make money. In Moyers’ words, “Arson is now a way of doing business in the South Bronx, encouraged and made profitable by well-intentioned public law.” Additionally, some fires were far less purposeful. According to Moyers, “Kids start a lot of the fires. They burn vacant buildings for the hell of it. A nine-year-old told me, “If you don't take my picture, I'll burn the building down." He meant it.”

8. Political Backlash Against Crime in Newark (1979)

Context
Deeper Explanation

11:32

This section references the mayor of Newark, Kenneth Gibson, who became the first Black mayor of Newark (indeed, he was the first Black mayor of any significant Northeastern city). He first won office in 1970, defeated Anthony Imperiale to win re-election in 1974, and ultimately held that office until 1986. During the 1970s and 1980s, dozens of cities across the country elected their first Black mayors, including Tom Bradley in Los Angeles, Harold Washington in Chicago, and David Dinkins in New York. These democratic trends owe significantly to the changing racial composition of American cities, and Black voters became larger proportions of urban electorates. However, these Black leaders gained political power at a particularly challenging historical moment, having to deal with the unenviable political task of dealing with the social problems associated with the urban crisis, including rising crime.

9. Stopping the “Vicious Cycle” of Suburban Flight (1976)

Context
Deeper Explanation

02:27

Suburban zoning policy—in New Jersey and elsewhere—frequently operated to discourage lower-income families from moving in. All cities typically “zone” different “blocks” within the community for certain kinds of activity; some blocks are for industrial activity, some for commerce, some for housing, etc. Importantly, the regions zoned for “residential purposes” often had particular rules about the kind of housing that could be built in that area. There could be rules about minimum lot size, the minimum square footage of a dwelling on a particular lot, rules that housing on a given lot must be for single-family homes rather than multi-family apartment buildings, etc. Through these mechanisms, suburban communities could effectively ensure that only middle- and upper-class families could move in. (For example, if zoning made apartment buildings effectively illegal, lower-income families would not likely be able to afford to live in that community). Thus, this reference to “zoning reform” refers to a state policy that would require suburban communities to adopt zoning changes that would allow for more low-income families to move into that community, which could help prevent poor people from being “trapped” in decaying cities.

Context
Deeper Explanation

03:15

The concept of “homesteading businesses” mentioned in this section is based on the premise that for cities to revive, more businesses needed to return to the city to provide job opportunities for urban residents. If cities provided free land and tax incentives to these businesses, perhaps entrepreneurs would be more likely to pursue economic opportunities in cities rather than suburbs.
