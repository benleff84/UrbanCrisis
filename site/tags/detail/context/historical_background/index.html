1. The Middle Class Exodus

Historical Background

09:53

As this section implies, the postwar period saw a boom in automobile ownership. By 1930, over 40% of American households owned a car, often purchased from Henry Ford or other American car companies. Car ownership stagnated during the Great Depression and World War II, before growing dramatically in the postwar period. By 1960, more than three in four households owned a car; over 20% owned more than one.

Historical Background

10:48

Indeed, the rise of the automobile (and accompanying automobile infrastructure) was a major force facilitating suburbanization. So too was federal housing policy. The GI Bill made it far easier for World War II veterans (a major portion of the population) to secure a mortgage on a new home. And New Deal-era Federal Housing Authority also incentivized new home construction outside the urban core. The FHA insured home mortgages, but not in certain neighborhoods that were deemed "high-risk"--because of the color of these neighborhoods on FHA maps, this process became known as "redlining." As a result of this policy, it was difficult to impossible to secure mortgages in certain urban neighborhoods, but easier to get insured loans in the suburbs. Thus, federal housing policy help attract the growing middle-class out of the city.

11. Debating Aid to Struggling Cities (1978)

Context
Historical Background

25:55

The fiscal situation in Cleveland provided the impetus to this particular episode of The MacNeil Lehrer Report. The city had recently defaulted on its loans to local banks (the first major city to do so since the Great Depression), leading to a political crisis. Mayor Dennis Kucinich had publicly announced that he would lay off hundreds of government workers and increase taxes to stabilize the city’s fiscal situation. City councilmen said Cleveland should instead sell a city-owned electrical power plant, while unions threatened to strike if Kucinich followed through on the proposed layoffs. The political crisis was just the latest example of the economic crises that were facing deindustrializing cities across the country.

12. The Political Challenge of Reviving New Jersey’s Cities (1982)

Context
Historical Background

17:22

This section references a major influence peddling scandal at New Jersey’s State Housing Finance Agency, whose primary role was to provide low-interest loans to developers that would build housing for low and middle-income residents. In the late 1970s and early 1980s, journalists and government investigations found that agency officials took bribes from developers trying to secure loans for projects. The scandal led to the resignation of HFA's Executive Director, William Johnston. For more information, see the investigatory report compiled in 1981

In 1976, New Jersey voters approved a referendum that legalized casino gambling in Atlantic City, making New Jersey the only state outside of Nevada with casinos. Atlantic City had a long history dating back to the early 20th century as a gathering place for revelers thanks its boardwalk, restaurants, hotels, and nightclubs—and saw its fair share of illegal drinking and gambling during the Prohibition era of the 1920s and 1930s. Proponents of legalization in the 1970s thought casino gambling could create tax revenue for the state and contribute to Atlantic City’s economic revitalization.

The city of Camden joined Newark as one of the prime examples of urban blight in New Jersey. Historical Background

20:37

Like many states in the mid-1970s, New Jersey was in dire fiscal straits and the state’s public schools were underfunded. At the same time, New Jersey residents were frustrated by rising property taxes, the major mechanism for funding schools at the time. In this environment, advocates who had spent years fighting for a statewide progressive income tax finally won the day. They argued that the tax could help the state provide more funding to schools and thus relieve pressure to further raise property taxes. They also argued that a state income tax was more equitable. It was structured as a “progressive” tax, so higher-income residents paid higher rates than low-income residents. And unlike property taxes collected at a more local level—in which wealthier localities could collect far more per capita revenue than struggling cities—a state tax could distribute money to help constrain the pattern in which suburban school districts were much better funded than poor urban districts. After much political wrangling, the New Jersey legislature established the new income tax in 1976.

2. The Struggles of the Auto Industry

Context
Historical Background

13:36

The shift towards more fuel efficient cars had two principal causes. First, the US experienced spikes in oil prices that resulted from foreign policy crises (for example, in 1973, Arab states put an embargo on oil shipments to the US in retaliation for American support for Israel in the Yom Kippur War). The skyrocketing price of gas meant US consumers were looking for cars that got more miles per gallon than the traditional Detroit-made “gas guzzler.” This is related to the second cause: import competition from Japan. Japanese automobile companies (such as Honda, Toyota, and Nissan) were producing affordable, smaller, more fuel efficient cars that were attractive to US consumers. As a result, US automakers lost considerable business. In 1965, the “Big Three” Detroit automakers (Ford, GM, and Chrysler) sold almost 90% of cars sold in the US; by 1980, that figure was less than 74%.

7. Fear of Crime in American Cities (1979)

Context
Historical Background

07:17

This clip documents fear of crime in Tucson, Arizona. Notably, Tucson is not an example of the “fading industrial center” discussed in much of this set; instead, it was a growing city in the “Sun Belt,” a region in the southern portion of the United States that didn’t suffer the same economic problems as the “Rust Belt” in the Northeast. While the population of many cities stagnated or shrunk in this era, Tucson was exploding: between 1950 and 1980, the city’s population grew from 45,000 to over 330,000, an over 600% increase. This shows that the rise of crime was not simply a mechanical result of economic dislocation associated with deindustrialization; the crime wave of the 1970s and 1980s had myriad causes. It also shows that social problems intensified in urban centers across the country, even in cities that were not facing significant economic hardship.
