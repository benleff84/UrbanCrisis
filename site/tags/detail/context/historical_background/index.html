1. The Middle Class Exodus

Context
Historical Background

09:53

As this section implies, the postwar period saw a boom in automobile ownership. By 1930, over 40% of American households owned a car, often purchased from Henry Ford or other American car companies. Car ownership stagnated during the Great Depression and World War II, before growing dramatically in the postwar period. By 1960, more than three in four households owned a car; over 20% owned more than one.

Context
Historical Background

10:48

Indeed, the rise of the automobile (and accompanying automobile infrastructure) was a major force facilitating suburbanization. So too was federal housing policy. The GI Bill made it far easier for World War II veterans (a major portion of the population) to secure a mortgage on a new home. And New Deal-era Federal Housing Authority also incentivized new home construction outside the urban core. The FHA insured home mortgages, but not in certain neighborhoods that were deemed "high-risk"--because of the color of these neighborhoods on FHA maps, this process became known as "redlining." As a result of this policy, it was difficult to impossible to secure mortgages in certain urban neighborhoods, but easier to get insured loans in the suburbs. Thus, federal housing policy help attract the growing middle-class out of the city.

2. The Struggles of the Auto Industry

Context
Historical Background

13:36

The shift towards more fuel efficient cars had two principal causes. First, the US experienced spikes in oil prices that resulted from foreign policy crises (for example, in 1973, Arab states put an embargo on oil shipments to the US in retaliation for American support for Israel in the Yom Kippur War). The skyrocketing price of gas meant US consumers were looking for cars that got more miles per gallon than the traditional Detroit-made “gas guzzler.” This is related to the second cause: import competition from Japan. Japanese automobile companies (such as Honda, Toyota, and Nissan) were producing affordable, smaller, more fuel efficient cars that were attractive to US consumers. As a result, US automakers lost considerable business. In 1965, the “Big Three” Detroit automakers (Ford, GM, and Chrysler) sold almost 90% of cars sold in the US; by 1980, that figure was less than 74%.

Joblessness and Lack of Opportunity for Black Urban Youth

Context
Historical Background

19:32

While Americans of all races struggled with unemployment in the early 1980s, the problem was particularly stark for Black Americans. According to Federal Reserve data, white unemployment was 9.1% in January 1983 (the year of this telecast), while Black unemployment rose to 21.2%.

Historical Background

24:05

According to census analysis by George Cave , 23% of Americans aged 16-19 who were in the labor force (i.e., not in school) were unemployed in 1982. For black male youth, that figure was 48.9%.

The Domino Effect of Plant Closures in the Rust Belt

Context
Historical Background

24:13

Steel industry layoffs devastated the tax base in small Mahoning Valley cities like Campbell. According to a 1983 Wall Street Journal piece, the nearby small city of Struthers closed its parks and swimming pool, and laid off ten full-time municipal employees. According to John Kovach, the city finance director, “we derive the majority of our income from the city income tax. And 50 percent of our workers aren’t getting income.”
