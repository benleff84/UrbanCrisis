The Struggles of the Auto Industry

The largest American urban centers of the mid-20th century were built around manufacturing, so these urban centers suffered when the industrial workforce started shrinking in the 1970s. During the 1970s and 1980s, American companies that made cars, steel, televisions, shoes, and many other goods lost market share to companies in other countries. As a result, thousands of factories closed. Perhaps even more significantly, advancements in automation meant that the remaining factories needed far fewer workers to achieve similar productivity levels. Detroit, which had risen as a center of the American auto industry, provides a prime example of this trend of deindustrialization. As seen in this 1980 episode of Bill Moyers Journal, thousands of auto workers lost their jobs as automation and global competition drove these businesses to restructure their operations. Increasingly, production that had once been done by unionized Detroit laborers was being outsourced to other countries or performed by robots. The steep rise in unemployment would devastate Detroit’s economy, and the impact on the city was calamitous: In 1950, Detroit was the fifth-largest city in the US, with 1.85 million residents. Between 1950 and 1980, Detroit lost one-third of its total population, and a million white residents left the city. Meanwhile, the suburban population of Wayne County lying outside Detroit nearly doubled. Thus, Detroit provides an extreme form of a pattern of deindustrialization and white flight that would play out in major cities across the country.

Annotations

13:36 - 13:43

The shift towards more fuel efficient cars had two principal causes. First, the US experienced spikes in oil prices that resulted from foreign policy crises (for example, in 1973, Arab states put an embargo on oil shipments to the US in retaliation for American support for Israel in the Yom Kippur War). The skyrocketing price of gas meant US consumers were looking for cars that got more miles per gallon than the traditional Detroit-made “gas guzzler.” This is related to the second cause: import competition from Japan. Japanese automobile companies (such as Honda, Toyota, and Nissan) were producing affordable, smaller, more fuel efficient cars that were attractive to US consumers. As a result, US automakers lost considerable business. In 1965, the “Big Three” Detroit automakers (Ford, GM, and Chrysler) sold almost 90% of cars sold in the US; by 1980, that figure was less than 74%.

15:31 - 15:39

As the interviewee indicates, longer-tenured workers often kept their jobs during layoffs because union contracts often protected laborers based on seniority with “last hired first fired" provisions. However, this meant a generation of younger workers lost their auto jobs early in their career; and furthermore, a generation of young men who would have gotten steady union jobs had they came of age in the 1950s were instead left struggling to find unemployment because they entered in the workforce in the “hard times” of the late 1970s

15:54 - 17:03

This section shows why even the “stabilized” US auto industry of the 1980s would never employ as many workers as it had during its mid-century height. While the cars produced by GM, Chrysler and Ford were technically “American-made,” many of the “pieces” of a car were actually produced in other countries. Between this globalization of the car supply chain and the automation discussed in the clip, far fewer American workers were needed to produce the same number of cars as would have been needed a generation before. This had devastating impacts on the economies of Detroit and other cities which relied on the tax revenue and consumer spending that came from employment in the auto industry.

17:29 - 18:05

This is a reference to the federal government bailout of Chrysler in 1979. The company was facing bankruptcy, and executives argued that the country had an interest in saving the jobs of Chrysler employees and protecting an American company from losing out to foreign competition. Ultimately, the federal government gave $1.5 billion in loans to Chrysler (which was ultimately paid back with interest).

Deeper Explanation

18:05 - 19:23

The discussion within this section illuminates the debates happening within unions in the late 1970s and early 1980s. Many workers felt that they were in a “catch-22” situation: if they fought company efforts to pursue globalization of the supply chain and automation, jobs might be saved, but the company might also go out of busines. If workers accepted these reforms, some jobs would be lost, but hopefully the company would stay in business and some jobs would be saved. In some cases, union negotiators made concessions to management that led to layoffs in order to maintain industry competitiveness, which led to some “rank and file” resentment of leadership. In the view of these critics, the union should be fighting harder rather than accepting the “management line” about the need to outsource certain parts of the production process.
